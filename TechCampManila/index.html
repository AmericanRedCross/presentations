What can GIS do for my organization?

Donor Engagement

  • collect project locations and map base infastructure
  • georeference photos
  • create maps for project donors
  • demonstrate technolgical sophistication
  • greater accountability

Historical Trend Analysis

  • record geography of current and past projects
  • long term perspective
  • evidence-based decision making for portfolio management

Managing Equipment

  • capture location, status, and coverage of response equipment
  • replace aging or inadequate equipment quickly
  • match distribution with vulnerability

Targeting Communities

  • map hazards and risks
  • research additional data (e.g. population)
  • identify activites by other organizations
  • select most at-risk and least-covered communities

Technical Collaboration

  • georeference and preserve project data
  • accessible, standardized data formats (csv, shapefile, Access database)
  • reuse valuable data from technical partners
  • share your data

OpenStreetMap

OpenStreetMap (OSM) is a project that is building a free map of the whole world. Thousands of members are teamed to create an accurate, detailed and up-to-date map that is as good or better than commercial products. Like Wikipedia OpenStreetMap benefits from the ongoing input of thousands of people around the world.
Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team
OSM data before and after - Tacloban, Philippines (Typhoon Haiyan)
OSM data before and after - Democratic Republic of the Congo (conflict)

community + open data + imagery =

29 hours for 68 volunteers to map an entire city
(20,105 buildings)

5 days for almost 200 volunteers to map 100,000+ buildings & hundreds of kilometers of roads

in Guinea where Ebola broke out. In just these few days since the outbreak, almost 200 mappers have made enormous progress filling in the map with the full range of detail needed by emergency workers, from major roads to individual buildings. The colors represent how recently each feature (building or road) was added to OpenStreetMap. Moving around the color wheel from red through yellow and green to blue, the features in red were added to the map as the first stage of the humanitarian mapping, and the ones in blue were mapped most recently.

Why use Mobile Data Collection?

FASTER

  • surveying and encoding combined into one step
  • data can be analyzed as it is being collected

More ACCURATE

  • skip logic to guide the data gatherer
  • constraint checks to ensure data is entered properly
  • no encoding step means no transcription errors

More INFORMATIVE

  • collect data at the source
  • GPS coordinates, timestamps, photos, etc.
  • better maps & reports
  • see the impact
  • because it's fun
