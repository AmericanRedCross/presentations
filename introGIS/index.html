Example uses of GIS:

  • Coordinating responders during disaster
  • Understanding existing resources and points of vulnerability
  • Highlighting the most affected areas
  • Visualizing logistical problems -- broken bridges, poor roads, etc.

Examples uses of GIS in Project Management:

  • Selecting project sites/li>
  • Overlaying secondary data unto project data
  • Visualizing trends over time
  • Combining participatory data from communities with technical data (environmental models, hazards)

Usages of GIS:

A sector-by-sector breakdown

Shelter

  • Identifying local construction material sources
  • Collecting and visualizing construction information at scale
  • Modeling disaster scenarios based on various shelter inputs

WatSan

  • Visualizing well overlap and potential water table drawdown
  • Modeling flood risk scenarios and computing impacts
  • Hydrological analysis: water sheds, basins, flow rates
  • Satellite imagery analysis to determine ideal well locations

Livelihoods

  • Correlating accessibility and livelihoods
  • Visualizing the spatial extent of cash distributions
  • Communicating demographics
  • Assessing landuse patterns and their correlation with income
  • Analyzing environmental inputs to livelihoods

Disaster Risk Reduction

  • Digitizing and analyzing community assessments
  • Improving technical collaboration between communities and outside agencies
  • Linking disaster models to early warning systems
  • More coordinated disaster planning and response

Climate Risk

  • Capturing and communicating forecasts and models
  • Cross-referencing outside data with RC data

    • Health

    • Mapping coverage / accessibility of clinics
    • Incorporating charts and graphs of epidemiological information into maps
    • Mapping the evolution of disease outbreaks and recognizing spatial trends
    • Cross-referencing RC preparedness data with oubreak data

    HIV

    • Mapping coverage / accessibility of clinics
    • Communicating safe spaces to stigmatized groups
    • Involving at-risk youth in mapping

    M and E

    • Mapping the spatial distribution of baseline data, indicators, etc.
    • Randomizing survey administration
    • Capturing and presenting photo, video and narrative data
    • Assessing spatial correlations in data
