Response at Haiyan Scale


Dale Kunce - Senior Geospatial Engineer

The first 72 hours were insane for GIS. We had to build an operational understanding of what happened and where so staff from the American Red Cross and other international Red Cross/Red Crescents around the world would know where to go and how to get there. I still didn't know the names of the islands.

coordination = +

spent lots of time coordinating in many different rolling skype and twitter conversations
we started asking for data, everyone was basically asking each other for the same data, it either didn't exist or it was in pdf
when we did start to get data it was often in pdf. PDF does not equal open data. Data must be machine readable processable and above all else require little to no work to reuse
We started mining websites and reverse engineering web services to get data. We used an army of volunteers, including the British Red Cross to free the data locked away in pdfs into geojson and other real spatial formats
Folks in the USG were awesome and always gave us great data in spatial formats we could use with open data licenses. When we got data either from the USG or other reliable places we would immediatly share it with the HOT team to prioritize tasks for not only the hardest hit areas but the areas where Humanitarian aid would be going first and then working out from there.
We started talking and embracing open data just over a year ago with a big commitment in the last 6 months. This event has proven and reinforced all of the reasons why open data is awesome. At SOTM US last year just after joining Red Cross I asked the OSM and larger geospatial community to help people help people. You guys lived it.
