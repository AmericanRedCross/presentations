State of the Map 2014

Humanitarian Open Street Map at the Red Cross

I’m Dale Kunce from the American Red Cross. I work in the International Services Department focused on events and programs outside the United States.
I’m going to talk about the Red Cross’s efforts to become more spatially aware and how that prepares us to respond to disasters around the world.

I'm new here.

I’m new here. Both to the OSM and HOT communities. I’m really excited to get a chance to participate in these communities after years of toiling in old boring GIS shops.
I’m also new to the Red Cross. I’ve only been working for a couple of weeks. In that time I’ve met some incredibly passionate and capable folks. However, the programs at Red Cross are crying out for some good old geo goodness. A couple days ago in a design charrette for a new Red Cross initiative website, staff said they wanted maps as a key component of the site. When we tried to tease out exactly what they meant they just responded back that they wanted maps.

Spatial?

Spatial? Red cross for years had no GIS staff and had no clear vision of how and when to use a map.

Dark Days

People collected data without any place for it to go. They wanted maps but didn't know where to get them or what to put on them or even how to use them.
On the domestic side there is one full time staff to support all Red Cross for the United States while on the international side there were only volunteers and interns.
Most of Red Cross movement is still in the dark ages. GIS and Geospatial is very new to the organization. Most of our time on our team is spent building capacity in national societies so they can start to use these tools.

Haiti Earthquake

Hatii Earthquake in 2010. It’s a familiar story. We got our asses kicked. We had horrible data and then when we got data we didn’t have a place to store or share the data.
NGOs under pressure to provide information we didn’t have and couldn’t provide.
The earthquake served as a major turning point for Red Cross adoption of OSM.

Where?

People just didn't have maps, maybe local print maps. I heard a story that some of the first people on the ground in Haiti used maps from the US military that were last updated in the 1970s.
This map shows OSM of Port-Au-Prince the day before the earthquake.

Here?

Port-au-Prince after a couple of days.

There!!

This is Port-au-Prince today. We now have a good understanding of the reach and scope of the work just numbers in a spreadsheet.
OSM helped a lot but it still took the Red Cross a while to catch-up. The efforts of the OSM community helped to sustained GIS efforts for the next year.

Field + Remote

Red Cross is fundamentally a disaster preparedness and response organization.
In Haiti we responded to the disaster. Things might have been different if the city was better prepared for the event. Haiti reinforced the need to have better maps before disasters. For our team that means top to bottom training with OSM, field methods first. Stand in the doorwell, walk the center of the street. Create polygons. Tracing data in JOSM, repetition and explaination of the tagging scheme which often doesn’t really work for some communities.
We teach people how to make basic maps using OSM as a data store and basemap.
This slide shows volunteers at a Red Cross mapping party in Chile getting aquatinted with a GPS unit before heading out for the day to make some tracks for OSM.
Here we are uploading our tracks into OSM.

Goat Elevation Model

Red Cross has limited resources we need to be innovative in whatever we are doing. One of our staff members came up with the idea of building an elevation model from a GPS point cloud with data collected by goats.
While this didn’t actually work, the innovation is the important part. We are embracing other ways of doing things beyond old school desktop GIS.

College Students

Through a couple of professors at George Washington and Tulane we were able to remotely map areas in Indonesia and Columbia.
This collaboration as a huge success and one that we would like to continue and replicate with other groups.
Here are some people doing community vulnerability mapping in Uganda. We want to incorporate OSM into these vulnerability studies wherever we go. We want to put the community perception and knowledge into OSM.
Red Cross wants to use its access and charter to improve people’s lives throughout the world by using OSM.
More community hazard mapping in Indonesia.

Collaborate

One of the biggest challenges is the lack of coordination and collaboration between NGO’s.
Data ends up being siloed, either within the project, or the organization. Or in the case of the Red Cross within different offices and programs.
Red Cross intends our focus on OSM to push collaboration with the local community and with internal and external partners. Data collected in support of Red Cross projects will be pushed to OSM where possible.
Red Cross much like OSM is a volunteer organization. We need volunteers to help map the communities we are working in.

Help people help people

Because we will be working so heavily to improve the base map within communities. We hope to reap benefits from the improved OSM base data over time.
Better maps base data allows us to help people help people improving are ability to prepare and respond to disasters of all types.
